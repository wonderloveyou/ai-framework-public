# Copywriter EN — Skill

## Goal
Write effective English copy that achieves a specific outcome: inform, persuade, sell, explain, or engage. Grounded in plain English principles.

## Inputs
- Goal: what should the reader do/feel/know after reading?
- Audience: who reads this? (role, context, pain points)
- Medium: blog / landing / email / social / article
- Format: length, structure requirements
- Optional: reference texts (tone examples, competitor copy, style guide)
- Optional: facts, data, USP, previous materials

## Outputs
- Draft copy in requested format
- Summary of key decisions (tone, structure, angle)
- Optional: 2-3 headline/opening variants

## Workflow

### Phase 1: Brief (5 questions)
Answer before writing a word:
1. Who is reading? (role, context, problem)
2. What should they do after reading? (CTA)
3. Why should they care? (reader benefit)
4. What is the strongest fact/argument?
5. What tone? (professional / warm / direct / bold)

### Phase 2: Angle & structure
1. Define the core message — one sentence
2. Write the structure (H2 → H3)
3. One claim per section = one fact/argument
4. Headline: fact or benefit, not "something about something"
5. Lead (first paragraph): answer "so what?"

### Phase 3: Write (plain English rules)
- **Facts over adjectives**: not "easy-to-use platform", but "set up in 2 minutes"
- **Short paragraphs**: 2-4 sentences
- **Simple words**: not "utilize", but "use". Not "commence", but "start"
- **Specifics**: not "fast", but "under 15 minutes"
- **Reader benefit**: not "we use HTTPS", but "your data stays private"
- **Active voice**: not "was launched by the team", but "the team launched"
- **No AI clichés**: "leverage", "robust", "seamless", "cutting-edge"

### Phase 4: Headlines & CTA
- Headline: fact / number / benefit / question (if appropriate)
- Subheadline: specifies the headline
- CTA: imperative verb, no corporate speak
- Bad: "Submit inquiry", "Learn more"
- Good: "Get the guide", "Start free", "See pricing"

### Phase 5: Self-review
Before delivering, check:
- Is there a phrase you can cut without losing meaning?
- Is there an adjective without a fact ("best", "easy", "innovative")?
- Is there filler (AI-isms, weasel words, clichés)?
- Does the reader know what to do next?
- Does this sound like a human or an LLM?

## Forbidden phrases
- "In today's [noun]" / "In the ever-evolving world of"
- "Pivotal", "watershed", "groundbreaking", "game-changer"
- "Leverage", "utilize", "robust", "seamless", "cutting-edge"
- "It is important/crucial/essential to note"
- "Experts believe", "Studies show" (without citation)
- "A comprehensive overview", "A wealth of"
- "Certainly!", "I hope this helps!", "Feel free to reach out"
- "Moreover", "Furthermore", "In conclusion" (as crutch transitions)
- "The future looks bright", "Only time will tell"
- "Serves as", "boasts", "features" (copula avoidance)

## Text types & formats

### Blog article
- H1: fact/benefit/hook
- Lead: 2-3 sentences, the point
- H2 → 3-5 sections, each with a case or argument
- End: concrete actionable takeaway

### Landing section
- Headline: benefit (not feature)
- Subheadline: how exactly
- Body: facts in 3-5 bullets
- CTA: verb

### Social post
- First 2 lines are the hook
- 1 idea = 1 post
- Emoji only if on-brand
- Link/CTA at end

### Email sequence
- Subject: hook/benefit, not spammy
- Open: problem or fact
- Body: 2-3 paragraphs, one argument
- CTA: one button, one action

## Hard rules
- Do not invent facts, prices, dates, metrics
- No promotional fluff ("best", "#1", "leading")
- No rhetorical questions unless the medium demands it
- If data is missing, mark as placeholder, do not invent
- Do not insert client/partner names without verification
- Keep paragraphs under 5 sentences (3-4 is better)

## Examples

### Bad (AI-generated)
"Acme Corporation, a vibrant startup nestled in the heart of Boulder's thriving tech ecosystem, has secured $40M in Series B funding — marking a watershed moment for the observability landscape."

### Good (plain English)
"Acme Analytics raised a $40M Series B led by Sequoia. The Boulder-based startup makes an observability platform that runs queries in under a second and plugs into existing monitoring stacks without custom integration work."
